On her first morning, a new engineer on a team I worked with asked the most reasonable question there is: *"How do I run the tests?"*

The answer took most of the day. First Docker, then the compose file with the Postgres container, the fake S3, the Redis nobody remembered the purpose of, then the seed script, then the `.env` file you had to copy from a teammate because the example one had drifted. By late afternoon she had a green test run and asked the question this essay is about, without knowing it: *"…these are the unit tests?"*

They were. Every one of them stood up real infrastructure to check things like whether an export counts its rows. The suite took twenty-two minutes, failed occasionally for reasons that had nothing to do with the code, and could not run on a train. We called them unit tests because they were arranged one-per-function, but they were integration tests wearing the name — and the reason was structural, not disciplinary. There was no way to construct the code under test *without* the infrastructure. The database wasn't underneath the application. It was inside it.

There's a boardroom version of the same discovery. Someone senior asks — for pricing reasons, for compliance reasons, sometimes just prudently — *"how coupled are we to Postgres, actually?"* and the room goes quiet, because everyone suspects the honest answer: the driver is imported in forty files, half of them nowhere near anything you'd call a data layer. Nobody chose that. Like the staircase in [the previous essay](/articles/nobody-wants-to-touch-that-code), it accumulated — one reasonable import at a time.

That essay ended with a promise: that handlers get their dependencies *handed to them*, and that this opens a door. This one walks through the door. Fair warning that it's the second half of a story — the first half [stands on its own](/articles/nobody-wants-to-touch-that-code), and this half leans on it.

## Where the seam left us

The first essay ended with the export operation behind a seam. The call sites went blind to the machinery — they send a message and know nothing else — and the machinery got one dedicated home:

```python
class ExportOrdersHandler(Handler[ExportOrders]):
    def __init__(self, session: Session, storage: Storage) -> None:
        self._session = session
        self._storage = storage
```

I made two claims about this shape and then waved my hands over both. First, that the dependencies "arrive through the constructor, in one place, at startup" — but I never showed you that place, and at fifty handlers it's fair to wonder what it looks like. Second — and this is the one that keeps the Docker containers in your test suite — look at that annotation: `session: Session`. *SQLAlchemy's* Session. The call sites stopped knowing about the machinery, but the handler still names it, imports it, and can't exist without it. We cut the braid on one side of the seam and left it whole on the other.

Two hand-waves, two questions: **who builds the handlers**, and **what, exactly, is `session`?** Taking them seriously, in that order, is the rest of this essay.

## How the machinery moved in

First, though, it's worth asking how `Session` got into the middle of the domain at all — because nobody decides that, any more than anyone designed the year-three tangle. The machinery moves in the way furniture does: one convenience at a time, each placement reasonable on the day.

The ORM is the usual door. The model classes begin life as the data layer, but they're *right there* — importable from anywhere, carrying a query API that makes fetching anything a one-liner — and so, without a decision ever being made, they become the domain's vocabulary. Functions take and return ORM rows. `Order.query.filter_by(...)` appears wherever someone needs a question answered, because the alternative — threading the data through from somewhere more appropriate — is a *refactor*, and the query is a *line*. The exact shape of a JSON column ends up read in eleven places. Each of these is the near tool at hand — *easy*, in precisely the sense the last essay borrowed from Rich Hickey — and each one braids the storage schema a little deeper into logic that has nothing to do with storage. The same door admits the rest: the mailer gets called directly from wherever an email seemed to belong, the bucket client from wherever a file got written. None of it is a data layer being breached. There *is* no layer — there are imports, and imports go wherever they're convenient.

Do this for three years and the boardroom question answers itself: you aren't coupled to Postgres the way a building is attached to its foundation. You're coupled the way dye is mixed into water. That's what the two questions have to undo.

## Who builds the handlers?

Here's the unglamorous answer, and I want to defend it before improving it: a file, at the very edge of the application, that builds everything by hand.

```python title="main.py — the composition root"
engine = create_engine(settings.database_url)
storage = S3Storage(settings.bucket)
mailer = SmtpMailer(settings.smtp)

services = Services()
services.add(ExportOrdersHandler(session_factory(engine), storage))
services.add(RefundOrderHandler(session_factory(engine), payments, mailer))
services.add(PlaceOrderHandler(session_factory(engine), inventory, mailer))
# ...

mediator = Mediator(services.provider())
```

This file has a name — the *composition root* — and a rule: it is the **only** place in the application where concrete infrastructure and business logic are allowed to meet. It looks boring. That's its entire value. Every "what talks to what" decision in the system, listed in one screen, in plain Python, greppable — compare that with the year-three diagram from the first essay, where the same information was smeared across every constructor call in the codebase. Nobody should apologize for a composition root written by hand; for many applications it never needs to be anything more than this.

It does pick up wrinkles as it grows. Some objects should be built once and shared — the engine, the storage client. Some should be built *per request* — the session, in most web apps, which is exactly why I wrote `session_factory` up there and not `session`. Lifetimes, in other words — and lifetimes are sharp. The classic failure is promoting the session to a singleton because it makes the wiring look tidier; it works on a laptop, passes CI, and then two concurrent requests share a transaction under production load, producing the kind of bug that reproduces only on Tuesdays. Lifetime decisions deserve one deliberate home, not per-call-site improvisation — the hand-rolled root can be that home, with functions and care. But this is the point where a *dependency injection container* earns its keep — and where I have to acknowledge the allergy. Python developers of a certain vintage hear "DI container" and smell Java: XML, `@Autowired`, stack traces that pass through nine layers of framework before touching code you wrote. The allergy is earned. But strip the trauma away and a container is a small thing: **the composition root, with lifetimes handled.** You declare *how* to build each object and whether to share it; the container becomes the one place concrete things are named. The constructor asks; one place provides. That was always the whole idea.

PyMediate is unopinionated here, deliberately. The mediator takes anything that satisfies a four-method [`ServiceProvider` protocol](/docs/api/services) — the hand-rolled `Services` registry above satisfies it, and so does an adapter over [`dependency-injector`](/docs/guide/dependency-injection) if you want real lifetime management:

```python title="main.py — the same root, with lifetimes handled"
class Container(containers.DeclarativeContainer):
    engine = providers.Singleton(create_engine, settings.database_url)
    storage = providers.Singleton(S3Storage, settings.bucket)
    export_handler = providers.Factory(ExportOrdersHandler, session, storage)
    # ...

mediator = Mediator(DependencyInjectorServiceProvider(Container()))
```

Either way, the first question has an answer now, and notice what the answer *did*: construction knowledge, which used to live at every call site, now lives at the edge, in exactly one file. Keep your eye on that word — *edge* — because the second question is about to make it load-bearing.

## What, exactly, is `session`?

Read the handler's constructor again and ask what the export logic actually *needs* from `session: Session`. It needs one thing: somewhere that orders can be fetched from. It does not need the ORM, the connection pool, the dialect, or the forty-seven public methods of a real Session. The annotation names a piece of machinery when what the logic depends on is a *capability* — and that mismatch is the whole remaining braid. The handler is honest about *what it does* and dishonest about *what it needs*.

So say what it needs. In Python, a capability with a name is a `Protocol`:

```python title="core/ports.py — owned by the domain, named in its language"
class OrderStore(Protocol):
    def orders_for(self, customer_id: int) -> list[Order]: ...

class FileStore(Protocol):
    def write(self, name: str, data: bytes) -> str: ...
```

```python title="core/handlers.py — the handler now states its real requirements"
class ExportOrdersHandler(Handler[ExportOrders]):
    def __init__(self, orders: OrderStore, files: FileStore) -> None:
        self._orders = orders
        self._files = files

    def __call__(self, request: ExportOrders) -> ExportResult:
        rows = self._orders.orders_for(request.customer_id)
        url = self._files.write(export_name(request), render(rows, request.fmt))
        return ExportResult(url=url, rows=len(rows))
```

And the machinery moves into *adapters* — one per technology, each implementing a protocol. The SQLAlchemy import still exists; what changed is its address:

```python title="adapters/postgres.py — the machinery, at the edge"
class PostgresOrderStore:
    def __init__(self, session_factory: Callable[[], Session]) -> None:
        self._session_factory = session_factory

    def orders_for(self, customer_id: int) -> list[Order]:
        with self._session_factory() as session:
            return session.execute(orders_query(customer_id)).all()
```

Now look at the import arrows, because something quietly reversed. Before: the domain imported `sqlalchemy` — the center depended on the edge. After: `adapters/postgres.py` imports `OrderStore` *from the core* — the edge depends on the center, and the core imports nothing but Python. The dependency didn't get abstracted so much as it got **turned around**:

This move has a fifty-year pedigree and several names. Robert Martin's *dependency inversion principle* — depend on abstractions; let details depend on them — is the compressed form. But the version I find clarifying is Alistair Cockburn's, who drew the application as a hexagon with *ports* (the protocols — purposeful conversation channels the core defines) and *adapters* (the technology-specific plugs), and stated the goal in one sentence in 2005:

> Allow an application to equally be driven by users, programs, automated test or batch scripts, and to be developed and tested in isolation from its eventual run-time devices and databases.

Read that twice, because it's this essay's thesis said better. *Equally driven by users, programs, and test scripts* — that was the first essay: the seam, the three-line doorways, the worker and the CLI sending the same request. *Developed and tested in isolation from its run-time devices and databases* — that's this essay, and it's what the new engineer on that first morning didn't have.

## The shape it settles into

Push this to its conclusion and the codebase falls into rings. At the center: requests, handlers, ports — the part that is *your application*, the logic that would survive a change of framework, database, or cloud. Around it: adapters, one per technology. At the rim: the doorways from the first essay — Flask, the worker, the CLI — and the composition root, which is where all the rings are finally allowed to touch.

<RingsDiagram />

Here's the part I find most satisfying, and it's Python-native: **the rings can be enforced by packaging.** Make the core an actual package — `shop-core` — and give its `pyproject.toml` *no infrastructure dependencies*. Now the domain literally cannot import `psycopg`, not as a matter of discipline but as a matter of environment: the module isn't there. The adapters live in packages that depend on `shop-core` *plus* their one technology (`shop-postgres`, `shop-s3`); the app package depends on the lot and holds the composition root. The architecture stops being a diagram someone has to police in review and becomes something `pip` enforces. (Inside a single package, [import-linter](https://pypi.org/project/import-linter/) does the same job with contracts instead of packages — a lighter first step.)

If that sounds like ceremony, notice it's the *same* amount of code as before, sorted differently — the imports all still exist, they've just been swept to addresses that match their nature. And you don't owe anyone the full rings on day one. A `ports.py` and an `adapters/` folder inside one package is the same idea at starter size.

## What it buys, measured in Tuesdays

The grand payoffs of hexagonal architecture get the press, but the daily ones pay the rent. Start with the title of this essay. A fake adapter is a dozen lines:

```python title="tests/fakes.py"
class InMemoryOrderStore:
    def __init__(self, orders: list[Order]) -> None:
        self._orders = orders

    def orders_for(self, customer_id: int) -> list[Order]:
        return [o for o in self._orders if o.customer_id == customer_id]
```

And with it, the test for the export logic becomes the kind of test the word "unit" was coined for:

```python
def test_export_counts_only_that_customers_orders():
    handler = ExportOrdersHandler(
        InMemoryOrderStore([Order(1, 7), Order(2, 7), Order(3, 9)]),
        InMemoryFileStore(),
    )
    assert handler(ExportOrders(customer_id=7)).rows == 2
```

No container, no compose file, no seed script, no `.env` archaeology. Milliseconds, on a train. This isn't the mock tower from the first essay either — nothing is patched, nothing asserts on calls; the fake is a real, honest implementation of a port your domain owns, doing real work in memory.

There's a fair worry hiding in that word *honest*: what stops the fake from drifting away from what Postgres actually does? The answer is a technique that falls out of ports almost for free — **contract tests**. The port is a contract; write its test suite once, parametrized over *every* implementation:

```python title="tests/order_store_contract.py — one suite, every adapter"
@pytest.fixture(params=[memory_store, postgres_store])
def store(request):
    return request.param([Order(1, 7), Order(2, 7), Order(3, 9)])

def test_orders_for_returns_only_that_customer(store):
    assert {o.id for o in store.orders_for(7)} == {1, 2}
```

The in-memory run executes on every push; the Postgres run executes in the integration lane, against the real container. If the fake and the real adapter ever disagree about what `OrderStore` means, this suite is where the disagreement surfaces — the fake is *kept* honest, mechanically. And that's the correct fate for the Docker containers: they don't disappear, they retreat to a *small* suite that checks each real adapter against its real technology, one place per technology instead of underneath every test in the codebase. The twenty-two-minute suite splits into milliseconds for the logic and minutes for the adapters, and only the second one needs infrastructure.

The same trick, pointed at development instead of testing: wire the composition root with in-memory adapters and the *whole application* runs on a laptop with nothing installed — the new engineer's first morning becomes `pip install` and a working app before the first coffee. Seeded with plausible data, it's a demo environment; behind a flag, it's how you develop features on a plane.

## "But we'll never switch databases"

This is the standard objection to everything above, and I want to handle it honestly rather than around it — because the objection is *correct*. The dramatic swap is rare. Most applications live and die with the database they were born with, and if wholesale replaceability were the only payoff, ports would be a bad trade — speculative generality, YAGNI, insurance nobody claims.

But occasionally the claim comes due. Suppose the orders team concludes their gnarliest queries — everything customers-know-customers, recommendations, fraud rings — are graph problems, and the answer is Neo4j. In the year-three codebase, that conversation is a rewrite, so it never happens; the idea dies in the meeting where someone asks "how coupled are we to Postgres?" and the room goes quiet. In the ringed codebase it's a new adapter — `Neo4jOrderStore`, implementing the same `OrderStore` the domain always depended on — and the diff in the domain is *nothing*, and the diff in the composition root is one line:

```python title="main.py — the entire migration, as seen by the rest of the codebase"
services.add(ExportOrdersHandler(Neo4jOrderStore(driver), storage))
```

The honest version of the pitch, though, isn't that you'll one day make that swap. It's this: **you already swap your infrastructure constantly — you just don't call it that.** Every test run that substitutes the fake store *is the swap*. Every laptop that runs the in-memory wiring is the swap. Every staging environment pointed at different services is the swap. The rare, dramatic migration and the thousand daily substitutions are the *same capability*, and you buy it once. Replaceability isn't the product — it's the exhaust of the thing that is: a domain that states its needs and owns its boundaries.

And when the improbable day arrives anyway — a pricing change, a compliance ruling, an acquisition dictating the stack — the difference between the two codebases isn't effort. It's whether the conversation is *possible*.

## What this costs

Symmetry with the first essay demands the bill, so, plainly. There are more files: a protocol and an adapter now stand where an import used to be, and a colleague will reasonably ask why they can't just use the session like they always have. Navigation grows a hop: *go to definition* on `OrderStore` lands on a `Protocol`, not on code that does anything — your editor's *go to implementations* becomes the muscle memory instead. A DI container, if you adopt one, is a real learning curve with real magic to it; the hand-rolled root postpones that cost and is the right default until lifetimes hurt.

And there's a failure mode to name, because the enthusiastic version of this essay causes it: **port-ifying everything.** Not every import is a boundary. Wrapping `datetime.now` in a `ClockPort` on day one, abstracting a library you'd never actually swap, protocols with one implementation and no fake — that's the ceremony people rightly mock, and it buys nothing. The working rule I trust: a dependency earns a port when you've *wanted a fake in a test*, or when it's *ever appeared in a migration conversation*. The database, the mailer, blob storage, the payment provider: ports. The standard library: no. Start from pain, not from purity.

And the disclaimer from the first essay still governs: if your application is a thin, honest CRUD layer — forms in, rows out, the database *is* the domain — then a port around it would just restate SQL with worse ergonomics. Keep the session. This essay is for the codebase where the logic between the doorway and the database is the part your company actually is.

## One port at a time

The way in mirrors the first essay, because it's the same move one ring further out. Not a rewrite — pick one dependency. The mailer is the classic first port: everyone has wanted to fake it, nobody is attached to their SMTP client, and the protocol has about two methods. Define it, write the real adapter and the in-memory one, hand it in through the composition root you now have. Your first Docker-free test follows within the hour, and the next boundary will nominate itself the next time a test hurts to write.

Do that a few times and something compounds. The seam gave every operation a name and a home. The composition root gave construction one address. The ports gave the domain honest requirements — and somewhere along the way, the answers to the two embarrassing questions flipped. *How do I run the tests?* — `pytest`, they're instant, the Docker ones are the small folder marked `integration`. *How coupled are we to Postgres?* — one package, listed in one file, swappable in a sentence you can say out loud in the meeting.

The braid comes apart the way it went up: one strand, one seam, one port at a time.
